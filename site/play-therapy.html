Play Therapy

Children don't need to find the words. Play is already their language. West Chester, PA — in-person and telehealth across Pennsylvania.

What is play therapy?

Play therapy is a form of therapy for children that uses play — the thing children do naturally — as the primary way of working through difficult emotions and experiences. It's not playing games to pass the time. It's evidence-based therapy that meets children exactly where their developmental capacity is.

Young children don't have the vocabulary or the cognitive development to sit across from a therapist and say "I'm feeling overwhelmed and I don't understand the changes happening in my family." But they can show you, through toys, art, stories, and pretend play, what's happening inside. A trained play therapist knows how to read that and respond therapeutically.

Play is a child's native language. Play therapy speaks it fluently.

Who play therapy helps

Play therapy is most commonly used with children ages 3–12, though the approach can be adapted for older children as well.

Common reasons families seek play therapy

  • Anxiety, fears, or excessive worry
  • School struggles, refusal, or social difficulties
  • Behavioral changes that came on suddenly
  • Grief and loss (death of a loved one or pet)
  • Family transitions (divorce, new sibling, moving)
  • Exposure to trauma or frightening events
  • Emotional outbursts or shutting down

Signs your child might benefit

  • They seem different since something happened
  • They're having a lot of big feelings they can't manage
  • They're withdrawn, or unusually clingy
  • Sleep or appetite has changed
  • They won't talk about what's bothering them
  • You've tried talking and it just makes things worse

What parents can expect

I know it can feel strange to drop your child off and not know exactly what's happening in that room. Here's what I want you to know:

Your child meets with me one-on-one — that private space is what makes it work. Children need to know their therapist isn't reporting everything back to their parents in order to feel safe enough to really open up. But you're not left out.

We'll meet periodically — without your child present — so I can share what themes are emerging in sessions and give you concrete things to try at home. I'll tell you how the work is progressing. You'll never be in the dark about whether this is helping.

The exception is safety: if your child says something that concerns me, I'll tell you. That's always the priority.

Play therapy is most effective when parents are engaged in the process. You don't have to be a perfect parent — you just have to be willing to be part of the team.

Questions about play therapy

  • What age is play therapy for?

    Play therapy is most commonly used with children ages 3 to 12. Younger children (3–6) especially benefit because they don't yet have the vocabulary or cognitive development to talk through their experiences the way adults do. Play is their natural language for processing the world.

  • Do parents join the sessions?

    Usually, the child meets with me individually — that one-on-one space is where the therapeutic work happens. However, parents are an important part of the process. We'll meet separately to discuss how things are going, and I'll share themes from the sessions (without breaking your child's trust) so you know what to expect at home.

  • How does play therapy help with anxiety?

    Children with anxiety often can't explain what they're feeling — it just comes out as behavior. In play therapy, kids are given space to act out scenarios, create stories, and express things symbolically through toys and art. This helps them externalize and process what's happening inside, in a way that feels safe and non-threatening.

  • How is play therapy different from regular child therapy?

    Traditional talk therapy assumes the client can articulate their thoughts and feelings verbally — something most young children can't do developmentally. Play therapy meets children where they are. The therapist is trained to understand the meaning in the play, the themes that emerge, and how to respond therapeutically without turning it into a lecture or a quiz.
