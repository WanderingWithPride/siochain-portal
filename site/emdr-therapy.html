EMDR Therapy

Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing — one of the most well-researched approaches to trauma that exists. West Chester, PA and telehealth across Pennsylvania.

What is EMDR?

EMDR stands for Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing. It sounds technical, but the idea behind it is actually quite simple: when something traumatic happens, your brain sometimes doesn't process it the way it processes ordinary experiences. The memory gets stored with all the original distress attached — and anything that reminds you of it can bring that distress flooding back.

EMDR uses bilateral stimulation — a gentle back-and-forth movement of attention, through eye movements, tapping, or sounds — while you hold the memory in mind. This seems to help the brain's natural processing system do what it couldn't do in the moment. The memory doesn't disappear, but it stops having the same charge.

It's backed by extensive research. The World Health Organization, the American Psychological Association, and the Department of Veterans Affairs all recognize EMDR as an effective treatment for PTSD.

EMDR is not hypnosis. You're fully awake and in control the entire time. Nothing happens without your awareness and consent.

Who benefits from EMDR?

EMDR was developed for trauma, but research has expanded its use significantly.

Strong evidence for

  • Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD)
  • Complex PTSD and childhood trauma
  • Single-incident trauma (accidents, assaults, medical events)
  • Grief and traumatic loss

Also used for

  • Anxiety and panic
  • Phobias
  • Depression rooted in past experiences
  • Medical trauma and health-related anxiety
  • Difficult memories that keep surfacing

What EMDR sessions look like

We don't start EMDR in the first session. Before any processing work, we spend time building stabilization skills — grounding techniques, a "calm place" resource, and understanding what makes you feel safe. This phase isn't optional, and it's not wasted time. It's what makes the rest possible.

When we're ready to begin processing, a typical session looks like this: you bring a specific memory or image to mind, notice the thoughts and feelings that come up, and then follow a bilateral stimulus (usually eye movements in-person, or tapping via telehealth) while your attention shifts. After a set, we pause and I ask what came up for you. Then we go again. Sessions are 50 minutes.

After EMDR processing, many clients describe memories feeling more distant — like they can look at them without getting pulled under. The facts don't change, but the emotional weight does.

Questions about EMDR

  • What does EMDR feel like?

    Most people describe EMDR as surprisingly gentle. You're not reliving the event — you're thinking about it while your attention is gently moved back and forth. Many people feel a sense of distance from the memory after sessions, like it lost some of its charge.

  • How many EMDR sessions will I need?

    For a single, specific event, some people see significant change in 6–12 sessions. For complex trauma or C-PTSD, it takes longer — often because we spend time building stabilization skills before diving into processing. We check in regularly and adjust as we go.

  • Is EMDR covered by insurance?

    Siochain Hope Counseling is currently private pay. I can provide a superbill you can submit to your insurance for potential out-of-network reimbursement. Many insurance plans cover out-of-network mental health services — it's worth calling to check your benefits.

  • Can EMDR be done via telehealth?

    Yes. Telehealth EMDR has solid research support. We use bilateral stimulation adapted for video — such as tapping or auditory cues — and research shows outcomes comparable to in-person sessions. You'll need a private space and a reliable internet connection.
