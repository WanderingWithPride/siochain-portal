Therapy for Chronic Illness

Living with a difficult body is its own kind of grief. This is a space that gets that.

The emotional weight of chronic illness

Nobody really talks about the grief that comes with chronic illness. Not just at the beginning — the ongoing grief. The things you had to give up. The future you had to revise. The relationships that changed, or didn't survive. The appointments that left you feeling dismissed. The exhaustion of having to advocate for yourself in every single room you walk into.

And then there's the invisible part: the way you can look fine to everyone else while running on empty. The guilt about canceling plans again. The anxiety about what the next scan will show. The identity questions — who am I now? Who was I before?

That's what therapy for chronic illness addresses. Not the medical condition — that's your doctors' territory. But all of the rest of it.

Why this matters to me personally

I'm not just clinically trained in this area — I have my own experience navigating chronic health conditions. That means when you're in my office (or on video), you don't have to explain what a flare day is, or why you cancelled your last three things, or why "just exercise more" made you want to flip a table.

I'm not going to minimize what you're dealing with or suggest you just need a better attitude. I understand the particular exhaustion of a healthcare system that often moves too fast, doesn't listen well enough, and leaves you doing emotional labor on top of everything else.

You deserve a therapist who gets it. That's what this space is.

Who this is for

Health conditions

  • Autoimmune conditions (lupus, MS, Crohn's, RA, and others)
  • Cardiac and cardiovascular conditions
  • Rare and undiagnosed diseases
  • Cancer — active, remission, or survivorship
  • Chronic pain syndromes (fibromyalgia, EDS, etc.)
  • Post-COVID and long COVID

Emotional experiences

  • Medical trauma (difficult diagnoses, procedures, hospitalizations)
  • Grief and loss related to illness
  • Anxiety about symptoms, tests, or the future
  • Caregiver burnout and support for caregivers
  • Identity disruption after a diagnosis
  • People in the diagnostic process — still searching for answers

What sessions look like

There's no script. We go where you need to go. Some weeks that's processing a difficult medical experience. Some weeks it's figuring out how to talk to a family member who doesn't understand. Some weeks it's sitting with grief that doesn't have a clean name.

I draw on trauma-informed approaches, somatic awareness, and cognitive behavioral techniques depending on what's most useful for you. If your chronic illness has involved trauma — frightening procedures, dismissive providers, near-death experiences — we can address that directly, including with EMDR if that feels right.

Sessions are 50 minutes. Telehealth is available, and for many of my chronic illness clients, it's the option that makes therapy sustainable on symptomatic days.

Common questions

  • Can therapy actually help with chronic illness?

    Yes. Therapy doesn't treat the physical condition — that's what your medical team is for. But the emotional weight of chronic illness is real and documented: grief, anxiety, depression, identity disruption, relationship strain, caregiver burnout. Therapy addresses those directly. Research consistently shows that people with chronic illness who have psychological support have better quality of life and, in many cases, better medical outcomes.

  • Do I need a diagnosis to benefit?

    No. Some people come in while they're still in the diagnostic process — which is its own kind of exhausting. Others are dealing with symptoms that doctors haven't been able to fully explain. You don't need a label to deserve support.

  • Is telehealth okay for this type of therapy?

    For many people with chronic illness, telehealth isn't just okay — it's actually the better option. On bad-symptom days, you don't have to cancel. You can attend from your couch, in your pajamas, under a heating pad. That flexibility is real and it matters.

  • How is this different from medical care?

    Therapy and medical care address different things and work best together. Your doctors treat the condition. Therapy addresses how you're living with it: the grief, the identity shifts, the anxiety, the relationship dynamics, the medical trauma that comes from years of navigating a system that doesn't always listen. These aren't overlapping — they're complementary.
